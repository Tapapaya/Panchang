## Problem Statement

The next generation of diaspora Hindus — people who grew up in the UK, US, Canada, or Australia — are losing connection to Hindu traditions. Not because they don't care, but because every existing Panchang app was built for someone who already understands the calendar system. They show "Ekadashi, Shatabhisha Nakshatra, Vishkambha Yoga" and stop there. That's a data dump for people with existing knowledge. The diaspora user needs meaning: what IS Ekadashi, why does it matter, what should I do today, what should I not eat, what shloka do I chant — and what does that shloka actually mean.

No existing app answers those questions. This one will.

## What Makes This Different

Every Panchang app assumes knowledge. This one assumes almost none.

The core insight: the diaspora user doesn't need a calendar app. They need a **daily cultural companion** that meets them where they are — with explanation, context, and the feeling that their tradition is alive and relevant today, not just a historical artifact.

The EUREKA from landscape research: conventional Panchang apps are built for India-based users. The diaspora user — second-generation, family memory but no formal training — is completely unserved. Their failure mode isn't forgetting to check the app; it's checking it and not understanding what they see.

## Target Users

**Primary:** Next-generation diaspora Hindus, 20s–40s, living outside India. Have family connection to tradition but little formal religious education. Speak English primarily. Know festivals exist but not always why or how to observe them.

**Secondary:** The builder's own family — an immediate test cohort who will give honest feedback and are the people the builder most wants to reach.

**Design principle:** Every screen should be understandable to someone who can't read Sanskrit and didn't grow up in a religious household. Assume English as primary language; Sanskrit as reverence layer (always with transliteration + translation).

## Constraints

- Solo builder, likely part-time
- Content curation is the bottleneck, not engineering
- Regional variation in Hindu practice is real (North/South, Gujarati/Bengali/Tamil) — must be acknowledged at v1 even if not fully supported
- Mobile-first (React Native), targeting iOS and Android

## Premises (all agreed)

1. The target user is a diaspora Hindu who needs context, not just calendar data.
2. A mobile app with push notifications is the right delivery for building daily habit.
3. Panchang calculation is a solved problem — use open-source libs, don't rebuild.
4. Content is the hard part. Curating accurate, explained shlokas and ritual guides will take longer than writing code.
5. "Community / open source" primarily means content contributions — shlokas, ritual guides, regional variations — not necessarily open-source code (though that's welcome too).
6. Regional variation must be acknowledged architecturally even if v1 covers one tradition.

## Landscape Research Findings

**What exists:** Drik Panchang, MPanchang, Shubh Panchang, Hindu Calendar 2026, Dharmayana, myMandir. Most offer tithi/nakshatra/yoga/karana calculations and festival lists.

**What's broken:**
- Timezone bugs (built for India, unusable in North America/UK)
- No explanation of what the data means — just the data
- Incomplete shloka phonetics (audio without matching text)
- Cluttered interfaces with too many features crammed in
- No step-by-step ritual guides
- Dharmayana tried the ritual angle but left mantras with incomplete transcriptions

**The gap:** Not one of them explains WHY. They tell you it's Ekadashi. None tell you what Ekadashi means, how to observe it, or what to chant. That's the white space.

## Approaches Considered

### Approach A: "Today First" — Minimal Viable Panchang
One screen. Today's date in the Hindu calendar, tithi, any vrat/festival, one relevant shloka with meaning, 3 dos & don'ts. Ship in 2-4 weeks.
- Effort: S | Risk: Low
- Pros: Ships fast; validates content format; tests push notification habit
- Cons: Thin until content fills in; requires manually curating 365 days upfront

### Approach B: "Tab Architecture" — Full Feature Set from Day 1
Four tabs: Calendar, Shlokas, Dates/Rituals, Today view. Full architecture, some placeholder content at launch.
- Effort: L | Risk: Medium
- Pros: Complete architecture from start; easier community framing
- Cons: 3 months before family can use it; skeleton ships before product

### Approach C: "Content-First" — GitHub JSON Backend + App Shell
Start with content in structured JSON files in a GitHub repo. Build app as a thin shell that renders it. Community contributions via GitHub PRs.
- Effort: M | Risk: Low-Medium
- Pros: Solves hardest problem first; GitHub as CMS is natural for open source
- Cons: No app to show for 2-3 weeks; JSON doesn't feel sacred enough for scholars

## Recommended Approach: A+C Hybrid

**Build the TODAY screen (Approach A speed) on top of a GitHub JSON content layer (Approach C architecture).**

Why this wins: speed and the right architecture are not in tension here. The content schema designed now prevents a painful refactor at month 3. The TODAY screen ships in 2-4 weeks. Contributions can start the day the repo is public.

### v1 Scope (Today Screen Only)

**v1 ships ONE tab: TODAY.** The 4-tab architecture is the target state; the tabs are built one at a time. Effort estimate of S/2-4 weeks applies to v1 (TODAY tab only):
- Tab 1: TODAY — v1 ✅
- Tab 2: CALENDAR — v2
- Tab 3: SHLOKAS — v2
- Tab 4: FESTIVALS — v2

This is not compromise; it's sequencing. The TODAY tab exercises the entire content schema and the Panchang calculation layer. If those work, the other tabs are rendering the same data differently.

### Architecture

**Panchang calculation library:** The JS/React Native ecosystem does not have a well-adopted, production-ready Panchang library. Recommended path:

1. **Try first:** The [`drik-panchang`](https://www.npmjs.com/package/drik-panchang) npm package (verify it exists and is maintained) or `astronomia` + a custom Panchang layer on top.
2. **Feasibility gate (3-day timebox):** Test whichever library you choose against DrikPanchang.com for your timezone. Must correctly compute tithi, nakshatra, and sunrise for 5 known dates. If it fails this test, do NOT spend more time debugging — switch to the Prokerala REST API (free tier, no API key required for limited use, returns all Panchang elements). Cache Prokerala responses locally for offline use.
3. **Fallback:** If the npm library route fails within 3 days, use the Prokerala API with `expo-file-system` caching of pre-computed data for the next 90 days. This means the app has offline Panchang data for 90 days forward, fetched on first launch.

Do NOT use Python — the app is React Native. Timezone: use device timezone automatically via `Intl.DateTimeFormat().resolvedOptions().timeZone` — no user timezone setting at v1.

**Content layer:** A `/content` directory in the GitHub repo containing:
- `days/` — JSON files keyed by `{paksha}-{tithi}-{masa}` (e.g., `shukla-ekadashi-kartik`). These keys are RECURRENT — the same key applies every year. Year-specific overrides (when a nakshatra or special condition changes the significance) go in `days/overrides/{year}/` with the same key. Adhika (intercalary) Masa: keyed as `adhika-{masa}` (e.g., `adhika-kartik`); the app checks for an Adhika Masa entry first, falls back to the standard masa entry if none exists.
- `shlokas/` — JSON files, one per shloka, containing: ID, name, Sanskrit text, IAST transliteration, English meaning, context (when/why chanted), audio URL (optional — link to YouTube or Vedabase, do not host your own at v1)
- `festivals/` — JSON files for major festivals with multi-day ritual guides, step-by-step instructions, significance, and regional variations
- `schema/` — JSON Schema definitions so contributors know exactly what fields are required

**Content delivery and offline support:** Content is **bundled into the app binary at build time** (test that 90 days of JSON files don't bloat the binary past 50MB before committing to 365 days bundled; if so, bundle 90 days and fetch the rest on first launch). On launch, the app fetches a lightweight `manifest.json` from GitHub Pages to check for a newer content bundle. If found, it downloads `content-bundle.zip` in the background. Bundle structure: `/days/`, `/shlokas/`, `/festivals/`, `manifest.json` (with `version` and `min_app_version` fields). Failure modes: if the update download fails → use bundled content silently (no error shown to user). If the bundled content is empty or corrupted → show "Content loading..." and retry; do not crash.

**Minimum viable content for v1 ship:** 30 days of `day_entry` files covering all 30 tithis at least once, with complete content for the 5 most common observances (Ekadashi, Amavasya, Purnima, Navratri Day 1, Diwali). This is the gate, not 365 days.

**v1 regional decision:** v1 defaults to **North Indian tradition (Vikram Samvat, Amanta month system)**. This covers the widest audience in the diaspora. Content files include a `regions` field for future use; v1 ignores it and renders all content marked `general` or `north-indian`. A region selector is a v2 feature. Open Question #1 is now closed.

**App layer (v1 = TODAY tab only):**
- Tab 1 (v1): TODAY — hero view; today's tithi, nakshatra, any vrat/festival, significance explanation, relevant shloka with full Sanskrit + IAST + English meaning, 3 dos & don'ts
- Tab 2 (v2): CALENDAR — month view; tap any day to see its significance
- Tab 3 (v2): SHLOKAS — searchable library
- Tab 4 (v2): FESTIVALS — step-by-step ritual guides

**Push notifications:** Morning notification (configurable time, default 7am) on days with significance. "Today is Ekadashi — tap to see your guide." Implementation: Expo Notifications. Permission is requested during onboarding AFTER the user has seen the TODAY screen once — not on first launch. On iOS, the system dialog fires only once; showing it after the user has already seen value maximizes acceptance. Hindi/regional language notifications are v2.

**Content accuracy review process:** All content PRs require a `reviewed_by` field in the JSON (name + contact). At v1, the builder (Tapan) is the sole reviewer. A `REVIEWING.md` file in the repo lists the criteria for accuracy review. When a community contributor adds a shloka, they must include the source reference (scripture name + chapter + verse). Content marked `unverified: true` is accepted into the repo but not shipped in the app until a reviewer clears it.

**Community contributions — two paths:**
1. **v1:** GitHub PR (for contributors who know Git) — CONTRIBUTING.md with schema examples. A JSON validator in CI (simple `ajv` schema check) ensures PRs don't break the schema. For non-technical contributors at v1: email the builder directly with shloka content; the builder creates the PR.
2. **v2:** Google Form for pandits and elders (defer until there are active non-technical contributors; Zapier/Make integration is not needed until then).

### Tech Stack (resolved)
- **App:** React Native with Expo managed workflow (acceptable for v1; bare workflow migration is documented as a known future cost if advanced push notification config is needed)
- **Content:** JSON files bundled into the app binary; GitHub Releases for content updates without full app re-submission
- **Panchang calc:** Evaluate `hindu-calendar-engine` vs `ephem` npm packages before starting; commit to one and document why
- **Push notifications:** Expo Notifications (handles APNs + FCM; sufficient for v1)
- **Distribution:** TestFlight (iOS) + APK via GitHub Releases (Android) at v1; App Store / Play Store at v2
- **Community contributions:** GitHub PRs for technical contributors + Google Form for non-technical contributors (pandits, elders)

## The Assignment

Before writing a single line of app code, do this:

**Step 1: Pick the Panchang library (3-day timebox).** Try `drik-panchang` or `astronomia` npm packages in a throwaway Node script. Test against DrikPanchang.com for your timezone: (a) today's tithi, (b) the next Ekadashi, (c) Diwali 2026. If neither works accurately within 3 days, switch to the Prokerala REST API and cache the responses. Document your choice and why before proceeding.

**Step 2: Create the JSON schema for a `day_entry` content file and write 3 complete example entries (North Indian, Vikram Samvat):**
1. A regular weekday with no festival (just tithi + a general morning shloka — Gayatri Mantra is a good default)
2. An Ekadashi day (include: tithi name, significance explanation in plain English, fasting instructions, what to eat/avoid, the Vishnu shloka associated with Ekadashi, the story of why Ekadashi matters — in 3 sentences, not a Wikipedia article)
3. One day of Navratri (multi-day festival — day number, the Devi aspect for that day, the associated shloka, specific ritual for that day)

If you can't fill in the fields confidently, that tells you exactly what content you need to gather — and who to ask — before you write a line of code. This is the real work.

**Step 3: Share the 3 JSON examples with one family member.** Not the app — just the JSON read out loud or pasted into WhatsApp. Does the content make them say "oh, I didn't know that" or "yes, that's right"? Their reaction tells you if the content format is working.
